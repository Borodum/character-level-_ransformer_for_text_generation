Across the suite of decoding strategies evaluated—beam search, temperature scaling, top‑k and top‑p (nucleus) sampling, mirostat, typical sampling, and pure random sampling—a clear theoretical trade‑off emerges between lexical diversity and structural coherence, governed by how each method shapes the next‑token distribution. Beam search deterministically selects the highest‑probability path, which maximises local coherence and adherence to training patterns but severely restricts diversity, as reflected in its tendency toward repetitive, low‑type‑token ratio (TTR) outputs. Conversely, temperature scaling flattens or sharpens the distribution; higher temperatures increase entropy and TTR at the cost of fragmenting narrative structure and elevating character error rates (CER), whereas lower temperatures suppress diversity but produce more syntactically plausible continuations. Top‑k and top‑p sampling introduce stochasticity by truncating the distribution, with top‑p offering a more dynamic cutoff that adapts to the confidence of the model—a mechanism that yields a more balanced diversity‑coherence profile, particularly in larger models where the probability mass is better calibrated. Mirostat goes a step further by dynamically adjusting the entropy target during generation, effectively self‑regulating to maintain a controlled level of surprise; this often results in a favourable middle ground, though it can occasionally produce erratic shifts in register. Typical sampling, which constrains choices to tokens near the expected entropy, tends to favour fluent but somewhat conservative outputs, leaning toward coherence over diversity. Random sampling, by ignoring the probability distribution entirely, maximises TTR and surface‐level creativity but at the expense of grammaticality and thematic continuity. When these same sampling strategies were evaluated on a larger, higher‑capacity transformer, the patterns held consistently: methods that introduced controlled stochasticity (e.g., moderate temperature, top‑p, mirostat) continued to offer the best compromise, while purely greedy or purely random strategies remained at opposing extremes. This convergence across model scales underscores that the diversity‑coherence trade‑off is fundamentally governed by the interaction between the sampling algorithm and the shape of the output distribution, rather than by model capacity alone.

When comparing the decoding methods directly, you can see a clear spectrum between diversity and coherence. Beam search sits at the coherence extreme: because it always picks the most likely path, the output is very stable and grammatically correct, but it often becomes repetitive and lacks variety, especially in longer passages. On the other end, random sampling ignores probabilities completely, so it produces highly diverse and unpredictable text, but the result is usually messy, with broken syntax and inconsistent ideas. In between, temperature scaling gives a basic way to control this trade-off—lower values behave more like beam search, while higher values increase randomness, though it doesn’t adapt to how confident the model is. Top-k and top-p sampling improve on this by limiting the pool of possible tokens, with top-p generally working better because it adjusts dynamically based on probability mass, keeping outputs coherent when the model is confident and more diverse when it is not. Mirostat takes this further by directly controlling the entropy during generation, often leading to a more stable balance, although it depends on tuning its parameters properly. Typical sampling leans more toward safe, fluent outputs by choosing tokens with expected probabilities, but it can feel a bit too conservative. When tested on a larger model, the same pattern still holds: top-p and mirostat give the best balance overall, while beam search and random sampling remain useful as clear extremes that highlight the core trade-off.
